A Veil of Meritocracy in Elite Higher Education

Is socioeconomic mobility into the American elite possible through our elite higher education institutions? Through what mechanisms are wealthy famillies able to confer major advantages to their children to maintain social class reproduction? This study dives into the role of social capital- who you know, cultural capital- you're ability to navigate highbrow cultural codes, and human capital- your abilities and skills- in explaining employment outcomes disparities by socioeconomic class.

This study confirms that socioeconomic status predicts employment outcomes. Furthermore, that the finance, consulting, and technology industries differ in class differences, as well as the role of human, social, and cultural capital.

Context

The meritocratic American dream implies that “if you’re smart enough and if you work hard enough", you can secure a better future for yourself in this country. Literature on this topic has largely focused on underprivileged students securing positions in universities, but few studies look into socioeconomic mobility from higher education into the elite class.


The meritocratic class has mastered the old trick of consolidating wealth

The employment rates of low socioeconomic status students in elite colleges who enter elite professions within finance, consulting, and tech may seem irrelevant to the vast majority of working class Americans. However, the entry level salary places these new graduates in the top 10 percentile of household income. This has key implications. Recently, researchers cite the top ten percent of the income distribution as disproportionately increasing its wealth and driving inequality. Furthermore, these entry level positions pave careers into powerful government and corporate positions. These professions are therefore considered a contemporary gateway into joining the U.S. economic elite.

This study looks at data collected at Dartmouth College (n = 395) to understand if the role of social capital, cultural capital, and human capital can explain the disparities in elite profession employment outcomes by socioeconomic class.

The Role of Social, Cultural, and Human Capital

Upper class parents confer employment and educational advantages to their children through social connections, cultural codes, and educational opportunities. Bourdieu extensively discusses the role of social capital - resources accessible through interpersonal connections, cultural capital - the ability to navigate upperclass cultural codes, and human capital- skills and talents.

Capital manifests in the family structure in a variety of manners. For example, family wealth becomes a form of economic capital for the child to inherit. Economic capital plays an especially important role in distinguishing children with elite families from children with educated, middle class families. For example, inherited economic capital enables elite children to develop expensive in-group behaviors, such as having financially exclusive hobbies and signal belong through status symbols. The role of economic capital in educational disadvantages is not only intuitive but well-documented, and therefore serves as the key independent variable used to predict employment outcomes.

An important role of social capital involves the transmission of information via social relationships. Information provides basis for action, such as knowing how to prepare for interviews or how to gain entry to exclusive networking events. Coleman (1990) theorizes that we should evaluate the structural and functional aspect of social capital. In other words, the social capital index should assess a person’s to social capital as well as whether they leveraged those connections into actual favors and resources. Many previous studies have managed to capture the effects of social capital on both educational outcomes and employment outcomes, but they do not sufficiently explain the full variation in hiring outcomes (Goddard 2003; Pager and Shepherd 2008).


More abstractly, the elite class’s preference for in-group culture is the underlying premise for why cultural capital plays such a big role in upward mobility. Cultural capital is, at best, vaguely defined as "transmissible parental cultural codes and practices capable of securing a return to their holders (Tzanakis 2011). Collins’ (1971) theory on educational stratification and disparities in job outcomes explains that high status groups construct employment requirements to monopolize the available opportunities through their cultural standards. As a result, the exclusion of people who lack in-group culture is legitimized and normalized, when in reality these non-cognitive personality traits have nothing to do with meritocratic skill and ability (Bourdieu and Passeron 1990; Collins 1971; Bourdieu and Passeron 1977). For example, Rivera identified the empirical effects of high-brow culture signals through a resume audit experiment. She found that the higher class resume (e.g. sailing, classical music) received law firm interviews at a 16.25 percent rate as compared to 1.28 percent for the lower class resume (track and field, country music), controlling for gender and identical academic achievements.

Results

Overall, this study set out to answer 3 main research questions:

1. What kinds of disparities by socioeconomic class exist in selective employment outcomes from elite higher education institutions?
2. What role does social, cultural, and human capital play in selective employment outcomes? Do these forms of capital explain class differences in achievement?
3. Does the role of social, cultural, and human capital differ within the finance, consulting, and technology industries?

Dartmouth students of lower socioeconomic status are less likely to be employed and selectively employed.

Are these differences significant?



Employment data was determined by asking college senior respondents to describe their post-graduate plans as of March 2019. This resulted in sample bias in which "Unemployment" refers to students who were not employed as of March. "Selective Employment" was determined by prestige rankings within each industry.

Accounting for cumulative GPA, social capital that enables pre-professional favors, and having a parent in the industry explain away the differences in class achievement of employment and selective employment.

Including all capital variables along with gender and race demographic controls allowed us to determined which forms of capital reduce and therefore likely explain the original gap in achievement by socioeconomic class.



All odds ratio values are to be interpreted in relation to the baseline (OR = 1), which would indicate no difference in employment outcome with an increase in the given variable. Values shown in purple with a (p>0.10) are not statistically significant.

Students from higher socioeconomic backgrounds are more likely to enter the finance industry in comparison to non-elite industries, with statistical significance.

Elite professional services follow the expected trend in which higher socioeconomic class predicts a higher likelihood of entering these industries. Descriptively, we observe the opposite trend with the technology industry in which lower socioeconomic status students are more likely to enter the technology industry.

However, when we analyze these descriptive differences we find that only the class advantage for finance remains statistically significant.

Descriptively, we find that students from higher socioeconomic backgrounds are more likely to enter finance and consulting than their lower socioeconomic background counterparts. The opposite trend appears true for technology employment.

All odds ratio values are to be interpreted in relation to the baseline group of other non-elite industries (OR = 1).

Class achievement differences in entering the consulting industry can be explained away by higher human capital, social capital, and cultural capital.

Having a higher cumulative GPA, larger professional network and tendency to ask for favors, and having highbrow hobbies all increased a student’s likelihood of entering the consulting industry over non-elite industries.


These findings corroborate existing research that the consulting interview process values merit through their resume selection process, skills-based case interviews. It also fits the emphasis on cultural capital that is often described by the "airplane test", in which interviewers look for candidates that they would enjoy as co-workers and friends.

Class achievement differences in entering the technology industry can be explained away by higher human capital, higher cultural capital and a parent in the industry.

Having a higher cumulative GPA, a parent in the technology industry, and having highbrow hobbies all increased a student’s likelihood of entering the technology industry over other industries.


This indicates that having a parent working in the technology industry confers some large advantage on students that cannot be accounted for through the number of professional connections, highbrow interests, or cumulative GPA that a student has. One possible explanation to test is that students whose parents work in this industry confer human capital advantages such as STEM proficiency or a likelihood to major in a STEM subject.

Conclusion

  1. Students from lower socioeconomic backgrounds are less likely to achieve selective employment than students from middle and high socioeconomic backgrounds.
  2. Certain forms of capital predict a higher likelihood of obtaining elite professional services (finance and consulting) employment.
    • The role of social capital confirms past findings.
    • The role of cultural capital confirms Rivera's findings that high-brow interests play a role in explaining employment results.
    • Furthermore, these findings confirm that cultural capital plays a role in securing technology industry employment as well.
    • Human capital, as measured by the proxy of cumulative grade point average, predicted a higher likelihood of working in consulting or technology.
    • Parent capital, as measured by having a parent who works in the same industry as the student, increased a student's odds of working in consulting or technology.
  3. The technology industry, which has thus far not been explored in this realm of research, is not as meritocratic as it claims to be. Having high-brow cultural capital as well as a parent in the industry greatly increases a student's chances of working in technology.

How was this study conducted?

METHODS

Principal Component Analysis was used to create indices. Logistic regression was used to produce results that compare to baseline values.

LIMITATIONS

Key limitations include a non-representative sample of the student body, little external validity of results in other universities, and that surveys lead to a lot of social desireability bias.

RESPONDENTS

Data includes responses from Dartmouth class of 2019 seniors (n = 463 ). Survey was fielded by Qualtrics and respondents were recruited via e-mail. The response rate was 50%, while the completion rate was 85%.
